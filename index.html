1. Introduction

Global plastic production has continued to grow at an unprecedented pace, exceeding hundreds of millions of tonnes annually and showing no signs of slowing as demand rises across packaging, consumer goods, medical supplies, and industrial applications. In 2023, the plastic production in 2023 was approximately 436 million metric tons [xx], with only 9% of it being recycled [xx].

Within this vast volume, we bring our attention to polypropylene (PP), which is one of the most widely used polymers due to its versatility, chemical resistance, and low cost. Despite its prevalence, only around 3% of PP is recycled [xx], remaining as one of the least recycled plastics worldwide. Compared to PET or HDPE, which benefit from established collection and recycling streams, PP faces low market demand, sorting challenges, and material degradation issues, resulting in most being incinerated or disposed to landfills.

The large-scale disposal of PP plastic through incineration not only fails to maximise the potential for recycling but also contributes to broader environmental concerns. The need to tackle this issue has never been more pressing, as the global community continues to grapple with escalating plastic waste and the imperative to adopt more sustainable practices.

This broader issue is mirrored within The National University of Singapore (NUS), where a significant portion of plastic waste, especially from laboratories equipment, containers, and other consumables, is composed of PP. Yet, due to the same structural barriers seen globally, nearly all of this PP waste is sent for incineration rather than recycled.

Currently, the plastic waste generated from these labs, including the PP plastic, would have been processed through incineration.

Our industry partner NUS Sustainability Strategy Unit (NUS SSU), raised the issue that they were unable to figure out how to effectively address the issue of PP plastic waste, particularly in terms of recycling and finding alternative uses for this material.

Hence, we were presented with our initial problem statement:

"What can we do with the current polypropylene (PP) waste in NUS?"

2.1.2 Core Challenge

The core challenge with PP recycling is creating value for rPP as a resource - there is simply little economic incentive to recycle PP.

Instead of focusing solely on improving the recycling process, the goal should be to develop end-use applications and markets for rPP that recognise its value, thereby driving demand and making it a viable, sustainable material.

By creating value for rPP, we can incentivise more effective recycling, transforming PP waste into a resource that contributes to a circular economy.

2.1.3 Viable Methods for Handling PP Waste

To effectively address PP waste, our team explored and compared three viable methods for upcycling and reusing this material: sequestration, repurposing, and recycling. These methods were evaluated based on their potential for further reuse, which creates new value for the material.

Based on this comparison, recycling emerges as the most sustainable and value-generating approach for managing PP waste. Not only does it allow for the creation of new products, but it also promotes the circulation of materials, thereby contributing to a more sustainable, closed-loop system.

Therefore, focusing efforts on improving the infrastructure and processes for recycling PP is crucial for enhancing its reusability and maximising its potential as a valuable resource.

2.1.4 Evaluation of Potential Methods for PP Recycling

To further explore the possibilities for recycling PP waste, we narrowed it down to three potential directions for reusing this material within the recycling process: plastic sheets, pellets, and 3D printing filament.

We determined our next steps by assessing each recycling pathway through two key factors: infrastructure cost and value-add.

  • Infrastructure costs reflect the level of equipment, technical requirements, and operational setup needed to carry out each method, which directly influence how feasible it is for NUS to implement at different scales.

  • Value-add considers how much additional utility or market relevance is created when PP waste is transformed into a new material.

By comparing these two factors, we identified which recycling methods offered the best balance between practicality and the potential to generate meaningful impact.

Based on the comparison in Table 4, 3D printing filament recycling stands out as a promising approach. It offers high value in terms of creative potential for prototyping and small-scale manufacturing.

While plastic sheets and pelletizing each have their merits, they either lack the versatility or incur higher infrastructure costs that limit their applicability for smaller, localized recycling efforts.

Therefore, 3D printing filament recycling is a suitable solution for both educational and commercial applications. This method offers a high-value solution by directly contributing to creative applications such as prototyping, which has significant potential.

2.2 Our Solution

Our project aims to convert PP waste into filament. Unlike other recycling methods, filament aligns with rapidly expanding applications in prototyping, education, engineering, and design. This positions filament as the most feasible and impactful product.

2.2.1 Creating a Circular Plastic Economy

A key strength of our approach is that it enables PP to recirculate continuously within the same ecosystem.

Once the 3D-printed parts reach the end of their use due to design iterations, prototyping cycles, or simple wear, they can be recollected, shredded, and re-extruded into new filament. NUS no longer needs to constantly import filament for students to use in their 3D printing.

This system is particularly novel: A circular plastic economy is created within NUS, where the material can be recycled multiple times until significant degradation occurs [xx][xx].

This localised circularity, turning internal waste directly into internal resources, represents a meaningful shift from traditional recycling approaches.

2.3 Target Audience

We selected NUS's Innovation & Design Hub as our first target user. It is the central makerspace for NUS's CDE (College of Design and Engineering), where most CDE students visit for rapid prototyping purposes, primarily 3D printing.

Even after these prints are completed or discarded, they can be collected, shredded, and re-extruded to be fed back to the Innovation & Design Hub as recycled filament right back to the Innovation & Design Hub as recycled filament for use.

2.5 Analysis of Current Solutions

One particular comment from our interviewees was about the lack of reliable printing - notably, a concern about filament jamming caused by inconsistent filament diameter.

From the literature review, jamming is caused by either filament breakage or large deviations in diameter and ovality [xx].

A filament is deemed consistent if it has

  1. Minimal ovality throughout its entire length, and
  2. Low deviation of diameter.

This ensures smooth feeding into the extruder during printing. These two key metrics will be explained in greater depth in Chapter 3.1.

Concurrently, we researched current small-scale filament extruders - 3Devo (state-of-the-art), Filabot, and Felfil (Table 6). While most machines were suitable for producing PLA filament, they could neither produce rPP nor PP filament consistently enough to be used reliably.

It is worth noting that these machines regularly struggle to produce consistent filament. Research mentions that they require uniform input feedstock, thereby necessitating the use of pellets to produce consistent filament.

Only 3Devo, the state-of-the-art, could produce consistent PLA filament using non-uniform feedstock. However, the resultant diameter tolerance suffers more when non-uniform feedstock is used, which poses as a significant trade-off [xx].

2.6 Value Proposition

To further illustrate the problem-solution fit, we crafted a value proposition canvas to map our user needs to our product features.

Value Proposition Statement

For designers and engineers who use 3D printing in their product development, our product is a small scale plastic extrusion machine that can produce consistent, high-quality rPP filament made entirely from campus waste unlike small-scale recycling systems that struggle with extruding PP consistently.

We aim to create a circular plastic economy within NUS that continuously recirculates PP and significantly extends its usage life.

2.7 Scope of Work

To design our small scale plastic extrusion machine, we identified three primary processes of filamentation and studied them sequentially:

  1. Cooling
  2. Heating
  3. Feedstock Control

In the first semester, we focused on cooling, the most critical factor affecting the filament's consistency. The remaining two will be studied in the subsequent semester, after which we will consolidate our learnings and experimental results to design the complete small-scale plastic extrusion machine.
