1. Introduction

Global plastic production has continued to grow at an unprecedented pace, exceeding hundreds of millions of tonnes annually and showing no signs of slowing as demand rises across packaging, consumer goods, medical supplies, and industrial applications. In 2023, the plastic production in 2023 was approximately 436 million metric tons [1], with only 9% of it being recycled [2].

Within this vast volume, we bring our attention to polypropylene (PP), which is one of the most widely used polymers due to its versatility, chemical resistance, and low cost. Despite its prevalence, only around 3% of PP is recycled [3], remaining as one of the least recycled plastics worldwide. Compared to PET or HDPE, which benefit from established collection and recycling streams, PP faces low market demand, sorting challenges, and material degradation issues, resulting in most being incinerated or disposed to landfills.

The large-scale disposal of PP plastic through incineration not only fails to maximise the potential for recycling but also contributes to broader environmental concerns. The need to tackle this issue has never been more pressing, as the global community continues to grapple with escalating plastic waste and the imperative to adopt more sustainable practices.

This broader issue is mirrored within The National University of Singapore (NUS), where a significant portion of plastic waste, especially from laboratories equipment, containers, and other consumables, is composed of PP. Yet, due to the same structural barriers seen globally, nearly all of this PP waste is sent for incineration rather than recycled.

Currently, the plastic waste, as seen in Figure 2, generated by these labs, including PP plastic, is processed through incineration.

Our industry partner, NUS Sustainability Strategy Unit (NUS SSU), highlighted that while PP waste on campus can be readily disposed of through incineration, it is nevertheless a missed opportunity. SSU recognised that incineration is an effective end-of-life solution, but sought alternatives that could unlock value, reduce waste, and make better use of this abundant material stream. The real question they posed was: what more can we do with the PP plastic currently being thrown away?

Hence, we were presented with our initial problem statement:

"What can we do with the current polypropylene (PP) waste in NUS?"

2.1.3 Viable Methods for Handling PP Waste

To effectively address PP waste, our team explored and compared three viable methods for upcycling and reusing this material: sequestration, repurposing, and recycling. These methods were evaluated based on their potential for further reuse, which creates new value for the material.

2.2 Our Solution

Our project aims to convert PP waste into filament. Unlike other recycling methods, filament aligns with rapidly expanding applications in prototyping, education, engineering, and design. This positions filament as the most feasible and impactful product.

2.2.1 Creating a Circular Plastic Economy

A key strength of our approach is that it enables PP to recirculate continuously within the same ecosystem.

Once the 3D-printed parts reach the end of their use due to design iterations, prototyping cycles, or simple wear, they can be recollected, shredded, and re-extruded into new filament. NUS no longer needs to constantly import filament for students to use in their 3D printing.

This system is particularly novel: A circular plastic economy is created within NUS, where the material can be recycled multiple times until significant degradation occurs [10][11].

This localised circularity, turning internal waste directly into internal resources, represents a meaningful shift from traditional recycling approaches.

3. Design Methodology

3.1 Metrics of Filament Consistency

Commercial 3D printers are designed to work with filament that is 1.75mm in diameter and circular in cross-section. This geometry ensures smooth feeding into the extruder and predictable flow through the nozzle. Issues arise when the filament deviates from these ideal measurements:

  • Too thick: it may jam the extruder nozzle or increase back-pressure, disrupting the print.

  • Too thin: it may snap during feeding, or the drive gears may fail to grip it consistently, leading to under-extrusion.

As mentioned in Chapter 2.5, there are 2 key metrics that helps us quantify the consistency of filament:

  1. Ovality (cross-sectional variation)
  2. Deviation of Diameter (variation along the filament's length)

These metrics help us determine the quality and reliability of the filament. Although both relate to dimensional inconsistency, they describe different modes of variation and arise from different process causes.

3.1.1 Ovality

Definition

Ovality refers to how much a filament's cross section departs from a perfect circle at a single point along its length.

It is calculated by measuring the filament's diameter twice at one point, along perpendicular axes, and then finding the difference between the two readings.

Ovality = Maximum Diameter - Minimum Diameter (at one cross-section)

Underlying Cause: Anisotropic Shrinkage

Ovality is caused by anisotropic shrinkage of the plastic during cooling, which describes the uneven contraction of filament, leading to a slightly elliptical cross-section. This is the result of the high crystallinity of PP.

Typically, plastics with low crystallinity (Eg. PET and PLA) comprise mostly amorphous regions where molecules are irregularly arranged [14]. These regions will have minimal shrinkage during cooling, resulting in minimal distortion to the filament's shape.

However, PP has a semi-crystalline structure with a high degree of crystallinity (30-80%) [15]. Regions with crystalline structures have polymer chains that are more efficiently and tightly packed, tending to have a higher density than in the amorphous regions. As a result, significant shrinkage (1-3%) [16] occurs at the semi-crystalline regions during the cooling of PP from its melted state after extrusion, while less shrinkage occurs at the amorphous regions. This uneven, anisotropic shrinkage distorts the shape of the filament and increases ovality.

*rPP/PP material from injection molded products was referenced here as it is the type of waste feedstock raised by NUS SSU

From Table 7, compared to PLA—the most common 3D printing material—and PET, which is widely used for recycled filaments, PP exhibits a significantly higher degree of crystallinity and corresponding volumetric shrinkage, by up to eight times and ten times respectively. This makes cooling control far more critical when producing PP filament compared to more dimensionally stable materials like PLA and PET.

Solution

The uniformity and rate of cooling affects the degree of formation of the crystalline structures within the material [20]. Ensuring that the filament can cool uniformly, and at an even rate would reduce the amount of uneven shrinkage, leading to a lower ovality.

3.1.2 Deviation of Diameter

Definition

Deviation of Diameter refers to the changes in filament diameter along its length, even if each individual cross-section remains circular.

When the diameter is plotted along the filament's length, the data points scatter around the nominal 1.75 mm line—some slightly thicker, some slightly thinner.

Underlying Cause: Unstable Extrusion

Deviation of diameter is caused by the unstable and inconsistent extrusion of the plastic, which results in portions of the filament to drift above or below the ideal diameter.

This is mainly due to PP's complex viscosity being highly sensitive to temperature fluctuations [21]. Even small changes in melt temperature can cause large variations in viscosity, which directly affects the flow rate during extrusion; An increase in temperature leads to a surge in material being extruded, while a decrease in temperature causes significant under-extrusion. These flow instabilities ultimately compromise the tolerance of the filament's nominal diameter.

From Table 8, it can be seen that PP’s complex viscosity is about 5 and 60 times more sensitive to temperature changes compared to PET and PLA respectively. PP’s extreme sensitivity means that the melting and extrusion mechanism must be carefully designed and tightly controlled to prevent fluctuations in filament diameter.

Solution

Ensuring uniformity in the heating of the filament and stability in the extrusion process will minimize any sudden changes in the flow rate, reducing the fluctuations in filament diameter.

Focusing on these subsystems allows targeted design and process improvements to address the underlying causes and achieve low ovality and deviation of diameter. This led us to create a functional decomposition diagram that integrated these subsystems and demonstrated the interactions between components.

3.3 Functional Decomposition

The production of rPP filament can be divided into three main subfunctions: Melting, Cooling and Pulling.

Subfunction 1: Melting

PP feedstock is delivered into the heating mechanism. The mechanism melts the feedstock to form a continuous molten flow, which is pushed through the extrusion nozzle to form the filament. Temperature is regulated through a temperature control signal, ensuring uniform melting.

Subfunction 2: Cooling

The molten filament passes through the Cooling Mechanism, where it solidifies into rPP filament. The Cooling Mechanism should ensure uniform cooling at the desired cooling rate, which is crucial for reducing ovality.

Subfunction 3: Pulling

As the filament solidifies, it is drawn by the pulling mechanism and wound onto the spool via the spooling mechanism.

Two key feedback signals regulate this stage,

  • Diameter control—which monitors and increases or decreases the pulling speed to increase or decrease the filament's diameter respectively

  • Tension control—which maintains an appropriate winding tension to prevent deformation or breakage during spooling.

4.4 Methodology

4.4.1 Data Measurement

Our measurement subsystem, part of the diameter control system, required changes. The Felfil Spooler's built-in single-axis diameter sensor cannot capture asymmetric deformation (ovality), which is common in rPP/PP filament. This is illustrated in Figure 11 and 12 below:

Hence, to accurately quantify the ovality of the extruded rPP/PP filament, we used a vernier caliper to take manual cross-sectional measurements along the major and minor axes instead (Figure 13).

4.4.2 Data Collection

Our step-by-step method is stated as such:

  1. 1.2 metres of produced filament will be collected.
  2. Each filament segment is measured using a vernier caliper in two orthogonal axes at 10 cm intervals, yielding 20 data points per run.
  3. The experiment was repeated 3 times under the same conditions.

Multiple measurements were also taken at each data point to ensure the maximum and minimum cross-sectional diameters for accurate ovality evaluation.

To improve reliability and account for run-to-run variation, experiments were conducted in triplets to ensure consistency, reliability and reproducibility of results. This allowed us to detect and minimize errors, check for variability in data, and confirm that the observed effects were due to our modification of parameters and not random chance or external noise factors.

The average of these three runs was then treated as the ground truth for that cooling configuration and used as the primary basis for comparison across all experiments.

4.4.3 Data Processing

For the raw values we collected from each experimental run, we computed the following metrics:

  • Average diameter: calculated as (Dmax + Dmin)/2 for each measurement point.
  • Standard deviation of diameter: using the sample standard deviation formula: S = √(Σ(D - d)2 / (n - 1)) where D is the average diameter at each point and n is the number of data points (20).
  • Minimum diameter: smallest average diameter recorded across the 20 points.
  • Maximum diameter: largest average diameter recorded.

To quantify filament ovality, we computed:

  • Pointwise ovality: Dmax- Dmin for each point
  • Mean ovality: average of all pointwise ovality values
  • Maximum ovality: largest ovality value observed across the filament

4.5 Modification 1: Bladeless Fan Ring

4.5.1 Description

Following the baseline experiments using the default Felfil cooling configuration, we proceeded with our first modification to the cooling subsystem (Fig 14), corresponding to Concept A in the morphological chart (Chapter 3.5).

To experimentally validate that cooling uniformity is a dominant factor influencing filament ovality, the bladeless fan was selected for its annular airflow profile, which provides low-gradient, uniform circumferential cooling. It is a simple and relatively compact retrofit, making it easy to integrate into our control setup.

The fan was positioned so that its annular outlet enclosed the filament path between the extruder and the spooler (Fig 15), creating a more symmetrical cooling region.

With reference to the methodology in Chapters 4.1.3 and 4.1.4, we evaluated whether this increase in cooling uniformity would reduce anisotropic volumetric shrinkage and, consequently, result in lower ovality values across the 2m filament segment.

4.5.2 Results & Discussion

Figure 16 below shows the mean ovality trends for both the control (default cooling fan array) and the bladeless fan configuration across increasing wind speeds.

For the Control Setup, mean ovality remained relatively low at the lowest fan setting but increased consistently as fan speed increased. This trend indicates that stronger airflow applied from a single direction introduced greater cooling asymmetry, which in turn led to higher anisotropic shrinkage and increased ovality. As wind speed increased from 0.8 m/s to 1.1 m/s, mean ovality rose from 0.062 mm to 0.081 mm. A further increase in wind speed to 1.8 m/s resulted in an additional rise in mean ovality to 0.097 mm, reinforcing the observation that higher unidirectional airflow exacerbates filament cross-section distortion.

In contrast, the Bladeless Fan Setup initially exhibited the opposite behaviour. Between 0.8 m/s and 1.1 m/s, mean ovality decreased from 0.096 mm to 0.090 mm, aligning with our expectation that a more circumferential and evenly distributed cooling profile would reduce anisotropic shrinkage. However, at the highest tested wind speed of 1.8 m/s, mean ovality increased to 0.109 mm. This produced a non-monotonic trend that diverged from our original hypothesis, which anticipated a continuous reduction in ovality with increasing uniform airflow.

To understand this, we examined existing airflow studies on bladeless fans. These studies show that bladeless airflow is not truly uniform around the ring; rather, the upper and lateral regions tend to receive disproportionately higher air velocities compared to the lower portions of the annulus. This creates circumferential velocity gradients rather than a perfectly even cooling field (Fig 17).

At low to moderate wind speeds, this airflow pattern still improved cooling uniformity compared to the control. However, as wind speed increased, these inherent velocity gradients were amplified, causing certain regions of the filament to cool and crystallise significantly faster than others. This reintroduced localised crystallisation gradients, resulting in increased ovality once airflow velocity exceeded a certain threshold.

4.5.3 Conclusion 1

We concluded that while the bladeless fan provided marginally better cooling uniformity than the default setup, it was still insufficient for achieving consistently low ovality across all operating conditions.

Nonetheless, our results demonstrated that a more uniform cooling environment had a positive impact on reducing ovality of the filament. Hence, we progressed to the next modification, a wind tunnel, that further enhances the uniformity of cooling.

4.6 Modification 2: Wind Tunnel

4.6.1 Description

We advanced to the Wind Tunnel cooling system (Concept B) to eliminate the circumferential airflow inconsistencies identified earlier.

The wind tunnel confines and channels airflow through a ‘cooling chamber’, ensuring air moves smoothly along the filament’s axis rather than dispersing unevenly. This promotes consistent convective heat transfer across the entire circumference of the filament and reduces the formation of localised crystallisation gradients and hence ovality.

We 3D-printed a custom wind tunnel using PLA. Air is supplied by fan motors through four slanted inlet ducts, each oriented to guide airflow along the tunnel interior. This angled-inlet configuration increases the uniformity of the downstream velocity profile and helps maintain steady, axial flow over the entire length of the cooling zone. Figures 18-22 show the 3D-printed wind tunnel, its airflow-velocity simulation, and the assembled setup integrated with the control system.

Compared to the Bladeless Fan and the default Cooling Fan Array, this design provides a substantially more controlled cooling environment, optimised explicitly for reducing anisotropic shrinkage and achieving minimum filament ovality.

5. Evaluation

5.1 Evaluation Against Target System Specifications

Focusing solely on the cooling mechanism, our wind tunnel has successfully reduced the ovality of the filament produced to 0.063 mm at a wind speed of 1.8 m/s (Chapter 4.6.2). However, it has yet to achieve the target system specifications (Chapter 3.4) of:

  • Machine ensures consistent diameter of filament produced (Diameter Tolerance: ± 0.1 mm)
    (currently: 0.103 mm)

5.2 Limitations

  • 3D Print: Testing of the filament on a 3D printer was not conducted due to the high risk of jamming and damaging the machine.
  • Unmelted Plastic: There may have been unmelted PP plastic within the heating mechanism that affected the overall rheology and conveyance of the material to the extruder, which we were unable to detect. This could have caused unexpected and significant deviations in filament diameter.
  • Contamination: The Felfil setup lacked a melt filter, meaning contaminants may have clogged the nozzle and disrupted the flow of melted PP.
  • Cooling Temperature: We did not vary the temperature of the air blown due to complexity and resource constraints. This also limited our ability to develop a quenching system.
  • Crystallisation: We did not measure the filament’s degree of crystallisation because the required equipment was beyond our scope. This measurement would have provided deeper insight into our experiments. Therefore, we assumed crystallisation to be directly proportional and based the success of our experiments primarily on filament ovality.

5.3 Future Work

5.3.1 Temperature Controlled Cooling

Although the wind tunnel setup was designed to provide more uniform circumferential cooling, we were unable to control the temperature of the airflow. This may have led to sub-optimal cooling rates.

Possible improvements include implementing air-cooling systems, staggered cooling zones, or a cooling enclosure that minimises interference from ambient temperature conditions.

5.3.2 Deviation of Diameter

Heating Mechanism

We intend to design a heating mechanism that applies heat more uniformly along the heating barrel to ensure the PP is fully melted when conveyed to the nozzle. A more even thermal profile will minimise partially melted zones and fluctuations in melt viscosity.

Pulling Mechanism

As the pulling mechanism is the last line of correction for the nominal diameter of the filament, it is essential that it actively adjusts the pull speed to avoid over-stretching or under-stretching the filament, which would cause excessive thinning or thickening respectively.

We intend to design a feedback loop to control the pulling speed by measuring the filament along two axes. This would also improve our ability to track and correct filament ovality.

Spooling Mechanism

Spooling relies on tension to roll the filament into spools, which is standard for producing filament usable in 3D printing. However, excessive tension may cause the filament to bend or buckle around the spool, affecting diameter tolerance.

To address this, we aim to utilise a clutch system for the spooler to minimise tension variability.

5.3.3 Varying Feedstock

Feeding Mechanism

From Chapter 2.5, we understand that a uniform feedstock improves the consistency of the filament. However, due to the variability in shape and size that arises from shredded PP waste, we may encounter feedstock-related filament consistency issues.

Therefore, we intend to first design a feed assistor to ensure consistent flow of the feedstock, and second introduce backflow pressure within the heated barrel via melt filters. This would enable a more homogenous flow of melted plastic through the heated barrel while also filtering impurities in the plastic.

5.3.4 Systems Integration

Once we have designed and tested the individual components and mechanisms, we will integrate them in parts to minimise variability before conducting a full system integration. Throughout the process, we will carry out validation and verification tests.

5.3.5 EcoSpool Filament Pilot Programme

Finally, as we prototype and produce sufficiently large spools of consistent rPP filament, we will provide these spools to the Innovation & Design Hub for testing and evaluation.

6. Conclusion

In summary, we aim to produce a small-scale filament extruder machine to recycle and valorise NUS’s PP waste into high-value rPP filament. This filament will support engineering and design projects across NUS, establishing a novel Circular Plastic Economy within the university.

This semester, we focused on experimenting with the machine’s Cooling Subfunction and determined that achieving an even cooling profile is essential for maintaining consistent filament ovality. Through these investigations, we identified a wind tunnel as the most promising mechanism to ensure stable and uniform filament geometry.

Next semester, we will continue experimentation and prototyping across the remaining Subfunctions and Subsystems, working towards the full development of our filament extruder machine.
